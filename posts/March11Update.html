NEW STUFF

Rajin Shankar
Artist & Programmer

This week's efforts have been primarily focused on smoothing out the design and construction of the sewer / mine section of our second "level."

One of the weaker points of the first level, and something that we'll hopefully be able to fix with a later pass, is the lack of a strong sense of place. The city areas tend to blend together without much development as the player progresses through.

With these issues in mind, I've been trying to create more distinct visual landmarks to help areas feel more unique and memorable, even if it's just within the context of the sewer itself. Interesting pipe formations and small details foreshadowing the later, more thematically dense sections of the game will hopefully impart a better sense of the slugs' world and story.

While there isn't too much to show in game, we've been doing a lot of work ironing out the design of the new areas within the arena section that we introduced last week. With cannons, aerial combat, and giant drums and hammers we're excited to start putting something together that is truly memorable on both a thematic and mechanical level.

TWEAKS

Making a Mechanic Fun and Engaging

Another recent development has been the rethinking of the grapple mechanic introduced in the first part of the sewer section. A planned feature from the very beginning, we'd all started to become weary of the ability's shortcomings, both technically and from the lens of player enjoyment.

While we liked the ability to smoothly carry horizontal momentum and build up speed by chaining successive swings, we'd found that the timing window to do this successfully was simply a bit too unforgiving for such a simple mechanic. Players would routinely find themselves running into walls and falling short of jumps, creating an experience of intense extremes—when the grapple worked it felt great, but when it didn't the flow of the game came to an awkward, grinding halt.

At a loss for how to make the system more fun and engaging without redesigning all the areas that rely on it, we turned to other examples of grappling mechanics in 2D platformers to see how the problem was solved there.
Flinthook, a game whose central movement ability is a quick, satisfying hook pull, proved to be an invaluable point of reference for making something that was both engaging and compatible with our existing level designs.

The short boost from the player's position to the grapple anchor proved to be a great answer to fixing the cumbersome, unpredictable movement offered by our original swinging motion while not sacrificing the ability to smoothly fly through an area when executed with good timing.
This solution also opens the door for more verticality when designing grapple-centric environments, which before could hardly be anything other than completely flat without mixing in additional movement options.
